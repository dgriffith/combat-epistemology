# hamming — Reference

## Quality Criteria

- [ ] Domain is bounded specifically enough that "important" has clear meaning
- [ ] Important problems are assessed on impact, tractability, and neglectedness — not just "bigness"
- [ ] Current work is described in time fractions, not just listed
- [ ] The gap between important problems and current work is made explicit
- [ ] Reasons for each gap are diagnosed honestly, not rationalized
- [ ] Each gap results in a decision: close, justify, or investigate
- [ ] Decisions include specific next actions, not just intentions

## Hamming at Different Scales

### Individual
"What's the most important problem I could work on, and why aren't I?" Focus on personal agency, skill match, and fear/comfort diagnosis.

### Team
"What's the most important problem our team could solve, and why aren't we?" Focus on coordination failures, misaligned incentives, and inherited priorities that no one has re-examined.

### Organization
"What's the most important problem our organization could address, and why aren't we?" Focus on structural constraints, political dynamics, and the gap between stated strategy and actual resource allocation.

### Field
"What's the most important open problem in our field, and why isn't more talent working on it?" Focus on incentive structures, prestige gradients, and whether the field's reward system points at impact or at legibility.

## Anti-Patterns

### Hamming Theater
Going through the exercise, producing a nice list of important problems, and then changing nothing. The exercise is only valuable if it changes allocation. If you do hamming and nothing changes, either the gaps are genuinely justified (write down why) or you're performing reflection without doing it.

### Importance Inflation
Claiming everything you currently work on is "actually important" by reframing it. If your current allocation perfectly matches the important problems, you didn't need hamming — or you're fooling yourself. Genuine hamming almost always reveals uncomfortable gaps.

### External Locus
Diagnosing every gap as caused by external forces: "My manager won't let me," "The organization doesn't value this," "There's no funding." Some of these are real. But hamming asks you to check: have you actually tried? Have you proposed it? Have you tested the constraint?

### False Constraints
Treating contingent circumstances as immovable laws. "I can't work on X because Y" — but have you checked whether Y is actually true, or whether Y could be changed? Many constraints dissolve when you push on them.

### Perpetual Planning
Using hamming as a recursive avoidance mechanism: "I need to do hamming again to make sure I have the right priorities before I start." At some point you have to stop reflecting and start working on the important problem. Hamming is a one-time reorientation, not a lifestyle.

## Integration

- **hamming + referenceclass**: When assessing tractability of important problems, use base rates. "Can we really reduce churn?" becomes "What's the base rate for startups that successfully reduced churn by 50%?"
- **hamming + goalfactor**: If you're working on something unimportant, use goalfactor to find what underlying goal it serves. There may be a more direct path to that goal.
- **hamming + aversionfactor**: When diagnosing why you're not working on the important problem, use aversionfactor to surface the specific aversion. The reason "I'm not working on it" is often emotional, not logical.
- **hamming + innerloop**: After identifying the important problem, check your inner simulator. Does it feel right? If your gut says "yes, that's important, but..." — the "but" is the hamming diagnosis.
- **hamming + noticing**: Build the habit of noticing when you're spending time on something and a small voice says "this doesn't matter." That's a hamming signal.

## Meta-Note

The Hamming Questions come from Richard Hamming's 1986 talk "You and Your Research," delivered at Bell Labs. Hamming observed that many talented scientists spent their careers on minor problems while avoiding the important ones. The impact/tractability/neglectedness framework comes from the effective altruism community's approach to cause prioritization. The process description, examples, and quality criteria here are original to this project, not reproductions of Hamming's talk or any other source.
