# innerloop — Worked Examples

## Example 1: Project Estimate

**Input**: "We told the client the redesign will be done in 4 weeks. Innerloop check."

**Step 1 — Official Prediction**: "The redesign will be delivered in 4 weeks."

**Step 2 — Quiet verbal reasoning.** Stop thinking about why 4 weeks is reasonable. Stop rehearsing the task breakdown.

**Step 3 — Simulate Concretely**:
Imagine it's the end of week 4. Friday afternoon. The client is expecting delivery.

...What do I see? I see the design is done but the front-end implementation is maybe 70% complete. I see the team lead looking stressed. I see a Slack message that says "the API changes we need from the backend team aren't ready yet." I see us sending an email asking for one more week.

**Step 4 — Surprise-O-Meter**: If we actually delivered in 4 weeks, complete and polished, how surprised would I be? Honestly: 7/10 surprised. That's a telling inversion — I'd be *more* surprised by success than failure.

**Step 5 — Discrepancy**: Stated: "4 weeks." Simulated: "5-6 weeks, and the backend dependency is the bottleneck I haven't raised yet."

**Step 6 — Hidden Knowledge**: The simulator knows:
- The backend team is already behind on their own sprint
- Last time we depended on them, they were 2 weeks late
- The designer tends to do one more revision round than planned
- I've been mentally preparing the "we need one more week" email since we gave the estimate

**Step 7 — Revise**: Tell the client 5-6 weeks now. Raise the backend dependency immediately and get a commitment date. The simulator has correctly identified the critical path that the official estimate ignored.

## Example 2: Difficult Conversation

**Input**: "I'm going to talk to my co-founder about equity split tomorrow. I think it'll go fine."

**Step 1 — Official Prediction**: "We'll have a productive conversation and reach agreement."

**Step 2 — Quiet verbal reasoning.** Stop rehearsing your arguments for why the split should be 60/40.

**Step 3 — Simulate Concretely**:
Imagine sitting down at the coffee shop tomorrow. You say "I want to talk about equity." Watch your co-founder's face.

...What do I see? I see them tense up. I see them say "sure" in a flat voice. I see myself presenting my case and them going quiet. I see the conversation ending with "let me think about it" — which means they're upset but not saying so. I see a weird text exchange two days later.

**Step 4 — Surprise-O-Meter**: If it actually went smoothly and we shook hands on a split, how surprised would I be? 6/10. Moderate surprise. My gut doesn't really expect smooth resolution.

**Step 5 — Discrepancy**: Stated: "It'll go fine." Simulated: "It will create tension that goes underground and resurfaces later."

**Step 6 — Hidden Knowledge**: The simulator knows:
- My co-founder avoids direct conflict but holds grudges
- I'm planning to propose a split that favors me, and I know they think they deserve more
- The last time we discussed something contentious, they agreed in the moment and then brought it up resentfully months later
- I'm framing "it'll go fine" because I want it to, not because I believe it

**Step 7 — Revise**: Don't revise the prediction — revise the approach. The simulator is telling me the *method* is wrong, not just the timeline. Options: bring in a neutral third party, send a written proposal first so they can react privately, or explicitly name the discomfort at the start of the conversation ("this is awkward, and I want to make sure we both say what we really think").

## Example 3: Job Offer

**Input**: "I got an offer from BigCorp. Higher salary, more prestige. I'm going to take it. Makes total sense on paper."

**Step 1 — Official Prediction**: "Taking this job will be a good career move and I'll be happy there."

**Step 2 — Quiet verbal reasoning.** Stop listing the pros and cons. Stop thinking about what LinkedIn will look like.

**Step 3 — Simulate Concretely**:
Imagine it's your first Monday at BigCorp. You badge into the building. You sit at your new desk. You meet your team. You open your laptop.

...What do I see? I see a large open office. I see a calendar full of meetings I didn't schedule. I see people who are polite but not warm. I see myself at 6pm, still in the office, working on something that doesn't feel like mine. I see myself at month 3, competent but bored, telling friends "it's fine, the money is good."

**Step 4 — Surprise-O-Meter**: If I was genuinely energized and fulfilled at BigCorp after 6 months, how surprised would I be? 8/10. My simulator strongly predicts dissatisfaction despite the on-paper logic.

**Step 5 — Discrepancy**: Stated: "Good career move, I'll be happy." Simulated: "Competent but hollow. Trading autonomy and meaning for salary and prestige."

**Step 6 — Hidden Knowledge**: The simulator knows:
- I left my last big-company job for the same reasons I'm now ignoring
- The things I value most (autonomy, creative control, small teams) are exactly what BigCorp doesn't offer
- I'm attracted to the offer because I'm anxious about money, not because I want the work
- "Makes total sense on paper" is something I say when my gut disagrees but I don't want to listen

**Step 7 — Revise**: Don't take the offer on autopilot. The simulator is flagging a values mismatch that the spreadsheet can't capture. Investigate: Can the current role solve the money anxiety? Could I negotiate BigCorp's terms to preserve autonomy? Is there a third option I haven't considered because I jumped at the first shiny thing?
