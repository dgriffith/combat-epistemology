# noticing — Reference

## Quality Criteria

- [ ] At least two signal types (epistemic, emotional, social, process) were scanned
- [ ] Each signal was named precisely, not left as "something feels off"
- [ ] The first explanation for each signal was held at arm's length, not immediately accepted
- [ ] Smoothing attempts were identified — the stories the brain told to dismiss the signals
- [ ] Each signal resulted in either investigation or an explicit flag for later
- [ ] The noticing produced actionable output, not just meta-awareness
- [ ] The process didn't become recursive (noticing about noticing about noticing)

## Anti-Patterns

### Explanation Too Fast
Noticing a signal and immediately explaining it away. "I feel uneasy about the project — probably just anxiety." The explanation arrives in the same breath as the noticing, which defeats the purpose. The discipline is to sit with the signal for at least a few seconds before accepting any explanation.

### Meta-Bypass
Using the language of noticing without doing the actual noticing. "I notice I might be rationalizing" followed by continuing to rationalize. The words become a ritual that substitutes for the cognitive work. Real noticing changes what you do next; verbal noticing changes nothing.

### Over-Noticing
Noticing every micro-signal until you're paralyzed by meta-awareness. Noticing is a tool for catching important signals, not a state of perpetual self-surveillance. If you're spending more time noticing than acting, you've overcorrected. A few important signals per day is plenty.

### Noticing as Avoidance
Using noticing itself as a way to avoid the uncomfortable thing you noticed. "I notice I'm avoiding the budget conversation. I should spend more time noticing what's going on with that avoidance." No — you should have the budget conversation. Noticing is step 1; it's not all the steps.

### Performance Noticing
Noticing things in order to appear self-aware, rather than to gain genuine insight. Common in group settings: "I just want to notice that I'm feeling defensive right now" (said in a way that performs openness without actually being open). Real noticing is often quiet and private. If it always happens out loud, it might be social signaling.

## Integration

- **noticing + innerloop**: Noticing catches the initial signal ("something's off"). Innerloop investigates it ("let me simulate this concretely and see what my gut actually expects").
- **noticing + aversionfactor**: When noticing reveals a flinch or ugh field, use aversionfactor to decompose the aversion into its components.
- **noticing + doublecrux**: When noticing catches a belief you're holding without examination, use doublecrux to find the crux — what evidence would change your mind?
- **noticing + taboo**: When noticing reveals that a key term is doing too much work ("we'll be fine," "it's basically done"), use taboo to force precise language.
- **noticing + hamming**: Noticing can surface the moment when you choose comfortable work over important work. That moment is a hamming signal.
- **noticing + referenceclass**: When you notice yourself thinking "this time is different," that's a cue to pull up the base rate.

## Meta-Note

Noticing as a deliberate epistemic practice was developed across the LessWrong and CFAR communities, drawing on mindfulness traditions and cognitive behavioral therapy. The "ugh field" concept was coined by Alicorn on LessWrong. CFAR's "noticing confusion" technique and Eliezer Yudkowsky's "if you notice you're confused, you should update" are key precursors. This skill generalizes beyond confusion to all signal types. The process description, examples, and quality criteria here are original to this project, not reproductions of any source materials.
