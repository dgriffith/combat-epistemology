# noticing — Worked Examples

## Example 1: In a Meeting

**Input**: "We just had our quarterly planning meeting. Everything seemed fine. Noticing check."

**Step 1 — Meta-Attention**: Shift into observer mode. Replay the meeting.

**Step 2 — Catch the Flicker**:
- **Epistemic**: When the VP said "we're on track for Q3 targets," three people exchanged a glance. Nobody asked a follow-up. That's a signal.
- **Emotional**: When the new initiative was presented, I felt a brief sinking feeling that I immediately overrode with "sounds exciting."
- **Social**: The underperforming team's update was unusually short. Nobody asked questions. The group silently agreed to not probe.
- **Process**: The action items from last quarter were never reviewed. We skipped right to new plans.

**Step 3 — Name It**:
- "I notice the room performed consensus about Q3 targets without anyone actually agreeing"
- "I notice I felt dread about the new initiative and relabeled it as excitement"
- "I notice we collectively protected the underperforming team from scrutiny"
- "I notice we never checked whether last quarter's plans actually happened"

**Step 4 — Resist First Explanation**:
- "The glance was nothing" → Maybe. Or maybe three people know the targets are unreachable and nobody wants to say it.
- "I'm excited about the new initiative" → Check: would I volunteer to lead it? No? Then I'm not excited.

**Step 5 — Flags**:
- Flag: Q3 targets — talk privately to one of the three glancers
- Flag: New initiative dread — use innerloop to simulate working on it
- Flag: Underperforming team — someone needs to have the direct conversation
- Flag: Missing accountability — propose reviewing last quarter's action items at the next meeting

## Example 2: While Planning

**Input**: "I'm writing my plan for launching the product next month. Help me notice what I'm glossing over."

**Step 1 — Meta-Attention**: Read the plan as an outsider, not its author.

**Step 2 — Catch the Flicker**:
- **Epistemic**: The plan says "marketing will drive initial users." How? Through what channel? With what budget? The vagueness is load-bearing — it's vague because I don't know the answer.
- **Emotional**: I feel a small flinch when I read the "pricing" section. I wrote "$29/month" but I have no confidence in that number and I don't want to think about it more.
- **Process**: The timeline has no buffer. Every task leads immediately to the next. I *know* this never works but I wrote it anyway because the real timeline feels too long.
- **Process**: "Hire contractor for X" appears in week 2 but I haven't started looking for a contractor. I'm planning as if finding one is instant.

**Step 3 — Name It**:
- "I notice the marketing section is a placeholder disguised as a plan"
- "I notice I flinch at the pricing decision and want to leave it as-is"
- "I notice the timeline has zero slack, which means it's fiction"
- "I notice I'm treating 'hire contractor' as a zero-time task"

**Step 4 — Resist First Explanation**:
- "The marketing plan is fine, I'll figure it out" → That's exactly the smoothing. The plan works *only if* marketing works, and I have no marketing plan.
- "I'll find a contractor easily" → When was the last time hiring was easy? Never.

**Step 5 — Investigate**:
- Marketing: Block two hours today to write the actual marketing plan. If I can't, that tells me something.
- Pricing: Use doublecrux with myself — what's my real objection to picking a price?
- Timeline: Add 40% buffer. If that feels too long, use innerloop to simulate the launch.
- Contractor: Start looking now. Move "hire contractor" to week -2.

## Example 3: During a Difficult Conversation

**Input**: "My partner and I were discussing whether to move cities. I said I'm open to it. Noticing check."

**Step 2 — Catch the Flicker**:
- **Emotional**: When I said "I'm open to it," I felt a tightness in my chest. That's not openness.
- **Social**: I said "I'm open to it" because my partner clearly wants to move and I didn't want to be the obstacle. I was performing flexibility.
- **Epistemic**: I haven't actually thought about what moving would mean. "Open to it" is a way to avoid thinking about it, not a position.

**Step 3 — Name It**:
- "I notice I said 'open' but my body said 'closed'"
- "I notice I was managing my partner's feelings, not expressing mine"
- "I notice 'I'm open to it' is a non-answer that sounds like an answer"

**Step 4 — Resist First Explanation**:
- "I am open to it, I just need to think more" → If I were open, thinking more would feel neutral or exciting. It feels heavy. That's a signal.

**Step 5 — Flags**:
- Flag: Use aversionfactor on the moving question — what specifically am I flinching from?
- Flag: Have the real conversation — "I said I'm open to it, but I realize I haven't actually figured out how I feel. Can we talk again after I've thought about it?"

## Example 4: Examining Own Beliefs

**Input**: "I believe our startup will succeed. Noticing check."

**Step 2 — Catch the Flicker**:
- **Epistemic**: I believe it, but when I imagine telling a stranger our metrics, I feel a pre-emptive defensiveness. I'm already rehearsing why the numbers are misleading. If the numbers supported my belief, I wouldn't need to defend them.
- **Emotional**: The word "succeed" gives me a burst of motivation. That's suspicious — I might be clinging to the belief because it feels good, not because it's true.
- **Process**: I've been avoiding updating the financial model for three weeks. If I believed we'd succeed, updating the model would feel fine. Avoiding it means I suspect the model would show something I don't want to see.

**Step 3 — Name It**:
- "I notice I'm pre-defending against criticism of our metrics"
- "I notice I'm attached to the belief for emotional reasons"
- "I notice I'm avoiding the financial model, which is evidence against my belief"

**Step 5 — Investigate**:
- Update the financial model today. Whatever it shows, I need to see it.
- Use referenceclass: what's the base rate for startups at our stage and metrics?
- Use innerloop: simulate telling an honest friend our situation and watch what I *expect* them to say.

## The Ugh Field

An **ugh field** is a zone of avoidance that has become self-reinforcing. You flinch away from a topic, and the flinch itself becomes associated with the topic, making you flinch harder next time. Eventually, you can't even *think about* the topic without discomfort, so you route around it entirely.

Common ugh fields:
- Financial situation (especially debt)
- Health issues you haven't addressed
- Relationships that need difficult conversations
- Professional tasks you've been procrastinating on
- Emails/messages you haven't responded to

Noticing is the first step to dissolving an ugh field. You can't address what you can't see, and ugh fields are specifically designed (by your brain) to be invisible.

The move: notice the avoidance, name it ("I have an ugh field around my finances"), and then take the smallest possible action to break the flinch pattern. Don't try to solve the whole problem — just look at it for 30 seconds.

## Building the Skill — Practice Prompts

Noticing is a meta-skill that improves with deliberate practice. Try these:

1. **End-of-day review**: "What did I gloss over today? What did I avoid looking at?"
2. **Conversation replay**: "In my last important conversation, what did I notice but not say?"
3. **Plan audit**: "In my current plan, where am I being vague because I don't know the answer?"
4. **Belief check**: "What's one thing I believe that I haven't updated in over a year? What would I notice if I looked at it fresh?"
5. **Ugh field scan**: "What topics make me flinch just by appearing in this list?"
