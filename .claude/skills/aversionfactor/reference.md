# aversionfactor -- reference

## quality criteria

**excavation depth**
- [ ] Used the magic wand test at least 3 times
- [ ] Reached an aversion that feels like the real one
- [ ] Person resonates with the identified root aversion

**aversion validation**
- [ ] Acknowledged the aversion without dismissing it
- [ ] Recognized what the aversion is protecting against
- [ ] Avoided "you just need to push through" dismissiveness

**resolution appropriateness**
- [ ] Resolution paths address the actual aversion, not the stated objection
- [ ] Considered that the aversion might be correct (the action might be wrong)
- [ ] Offered multiple paths, not just "get over it"

## aversion taxonomy

### fear-based aversions

**fear of failure**
"If I try and fail, that means I'm not good enough"
- Reveals: identity is attached to outcome
- Resolution: decouple identity from outcome, reframe failure as data

**fear of success**
"If I succeed, expectations will rise / my life will change / I'll lose something"
- Reveals: success has hidden costs
- Resolution: examine what success would actually change, address the real fear

**fear of judgment**
"People will think I'm [arrogant/stupid/selfish]"
- Reveals: social self is threatened
- Resolution: whose judgment actually matters? what's the realistic consequence?

**fear of commitment**
"If I do this, I'm closing off other options"
- Reveals: optionality is being protected
- Resolution: is the optionality actually valuable? opportunity cost of not choosing?

### identity-based aversions

**"that's not who I am"**
"Doing X would mean I'm the kind of person who..."
- Reveals: action conflicts with self-image
- Resolution: is the self-image serving you? can you expand it?

**competence threat**
"I should already know how to do this; asking for help is shameful"
- Reveals: incompetence is identity-threatening
- Resolution: separate learning from failing; normalize not-knowing

### effort-based aversions

**activation energy**
"I know it's not that hard but I just... can't start"
- Reveals: the first step feels aversive even if the task isn't
- Resolution: make the first step trivially small; address environmental friction

**anticipated effort mismatch**
"It seems like so much work" (but when examined, it's not)
- Reveals: the task has accrued negative associations
- Resolution: break down actual steps; do one step to break the spell

### social aversions

**social exposure**
"I'd have to [ask for help / promote myself / be visible]"
- Reveals: social risk is the real cost
- Resolution: what's the actual worst case? can you do a smaller exposure?

**relationship threat**
"This might upset [person] / change our dynamic"
- Reveals: relationship stability is being protected
- Resolution: is the protection necessary? what's the cost of not acting?

### values-based aversions

**moral discomfort**
"This feels ethically gray / it's not how things should be done"
- Reveals: action conflicts with values (possibly legitimate!)
- Resolution: is the discomfort tracking something real? adjust action or accept tradeoff?

**authenticity violation**
"This would be fake / manipulative / not genuine"
- Reveals: action conflicts with authenticity values
- Resolution: is there a version of the action that feels authentic?

## anti-patterns

### surface-level problem solving
"You say you don't have time? Let's optimize your calendar!"
-> Solving the stated objection when it's a proxy wastes effort

### judgment
"That's a dumb reason not to do it"
-> Shaming the aversion drives it underground; it'll re-emerge as a different objection

### forced positivity
"You'll definitely succeed! There's nothing to be afraid of!"
-> Dismissing legitimate fears doesn't address them

### one-size-fits-all
"You just need more motivation/discipline/accountability"
-> Generic solutions don't address specific aversions

### premature resolution
Jumping to solutions before fully understanding the aversion
-> Take time to sit with what's actually there

## integration with other ops

**upstream:**
- **goalfactor** -> "do you actually want this?" should come before "why aren't you doing it?"

**downstream:**
- **murphyjitsu** -> once aversion is addressed, stress-test the plan
- **doublecrux** -> if the aversion is about a belief ("I'll definitely fail"), find the crux

**parallel:**
- **taboo** -> if the aversion is vague ("it's just hard"), force unpacking

## meta-note

aversionfactor = **resistance archaeology**.

It finds what's actually generating the "no" -- which is often something much more interesting and addressable than the stated reason. The stated objection is usually a bodyguard protecting the real vulnerability.

The aversion factoring technique was developed at the Center for Applied Rationality (CFAR), based on the observation that humans systematically misreport their objections. The process description, examples, and quality criteria here are original to this project, not reproductions of CFAR's curriculum materials.
