# taboo -- reference

## quality criteria

**successful taboo**
- [ ] The banned word does not appear in the unpacked version
- [ ] Unpacking uses concrete, specific language
- [ ] Hidden assumptions are surfaced

**diagnostic value**
- [ ] Reveals whether disagreement is verbal or substantive
- [ ] Exposes what each party is actually tracking
- [ ] Identifies reification or conflation

**completeness**
- [ ] All major components of the term are addressed
- [ ] Normative and descriptive elements separated
- [ ] Boundary conditions made explicit

---

## anti-patterns

### synonym substitution
Replacing "freedom" with "liberty" is not taboo -- you need to unpack what you're actually tracking. The goal is decomposition, not lexical swap.

### definition recitation
Giving a dictionary definition misses the point -- taboo asks what *you* mean, in *this* context. Dictionary definitions are generic; taboo demands specificity.

### scope creep
If unpacking one term requires unpacking five others, focus on the most load-bearing term first. You can always taboo additional terms in subsequent rounds.

### weaponization
Taboo is for clarity, not for making opponents look confused. Apply it to your own terms first. If you're only tabooing the other side's words, you're using it as a rhetorical weapon.

## integration with other ops

**upstream:**
- **excavate** -- when an assumption contains a contested term, taboo it to see what's really being assumed

**downstream:**
- **doublecrux** -- after taboo clears the semantic fog, find the real crux of the disagreement
- **steelman** -- taboo first ensures you're steelmanning the actual position, not a word-ghost

**composability pattern:** excavate -> taboo -> doublecrux is a common chain: surface assumptions, clear semantic confusion, then locate the real disagreement.

## meta-note

Taboo is **semantic hygiene**.

It doesn't resolve disagreements -- it reveals whether disagreements are real. Half of all "deep philosophical disputes" evaporate when you force people to say what they mean.

The concept of tabooing words as an epistemic technique was originated by Eliezer Yudkowsky on LessWrong, drawing on the logical positivist intuition that most metaphysical confusion is linguistic confusion in disguise. The process description, examples, and quality criteria here are original to this project, not reproductions of Yudkowsky's writing.
