---
name: taboo
description: taboo forces semantic decompression by banning a word and requiring its replacement with unpacked definitions. it exposes hidden disagreements masquerading as word-disputes, surfaces unstated assumptions baked into terminology, and reveals when two parties are using the same word to mean different things. the goal is to escape the semantic trap where confusion hides inside loaded, ambiguous, or reified terms.
---

## tl;dr

**taboo** bans a word and forces you to say what you actually mean without it.

taboo asks:

> "what would you have to say if you couldn't use that word?"

it's a **semantic unpacking operator**.

---

## when to use

use **taboo** when you notice:

- an argument that hinges on a **contested term** (freedom, fairness, intelligence, consciousness)
- two people **agreeing or disagreeing** but possibly meaning different things
- a word that's doing **too much work** — load-bearing but undefined
- **reification** — treating an abstraction as a concrete thing
- confusion that feels **linguistic** rather than substantive

don't use when:

- the term is technical and well-defined in context
- you want to explore opposition → **antithesize**
- you want to surface assumptions → **excavate**

rule of thumb:
**taboo = "if you couldn't use that word, what would you say instead?"**

---

## the mechanism

most arguments about "free will," "consciousness," "art," "democracy," etc. are not about the thing — they're about the word-boundaries. taboo bypasses this by requiring:

1. **ban the word** — it cannot appear in subsequent discussion
2. **unpack the referent** — say what you actually mean using other words
3. **compare unpacked versions** — now you can see if the disagreement is real or verbal

the insight: if two people can't agree on whether something "is art," forcing them to describe what properties they're actually tracking often dissolves the dispute or reveals what's actually at stake.

---

## signature

taboo(term, context?) → {unpacked_definitions, revealed_assumptions, residual_disagreement}

- **term:** the word to ban
- **context:** optional framing (whose usage? what domain?)

output is:

- **unpacked definitions** — what the speaker(s) actually mean
- **revealed assumptions** — implicit claims hiding inside the word
- **residual disagreement** — what real disputes remain after semantic clearing

---

## process (step by step)

### step 0: identify the suspect term
look for words that are:
- abstract and contested
- used by multiple parties with possible different meanings
- load-bearing in the argument
- emotionally charged or politically valenced

### step 1: ban the word
declare it forbidden. it cannot appear in the next round of discussion.

### step 2: request unpacked restatement
ask each party (or yourself): "restate your claim without using [term]. what are you actually pointing at?"

### step 3: identify components
break down what the term was bundling:
- **descriptive claims** — what empirical facts?
- **normative claims** — what values or preferences?
- **boundary claims** — what's included/excluded?
- **causal claims** — what mechanisms assumed?

### step 4: compare unpacked versions
if multiple parties: do their unpacked versions actually conflict?
if solo: does your unpacked version reveal hidden commitments?

### step 5: locate residual disagreement
after semantic clearing, identify what genuine dispute (if any) remains. this is the real crux.

---

## quality criteria

**successful taboo**
- [ ] the banned word does not appear in the unpacked version
- [ ] unpacking uses concrete, specific language
- [ ] hidden assumptions are surfaced

**diagnostic value**
- [ ] reveals whether disagreement is verbal or substantive
- [ ] exposes what each party is actually tracking
- [ ] identifies reification or conflation

**completeness**
- [ ] all major components of the term are addressed
- [ ] normative and descriptive elements separated
- [ ] boundary conditions made explicit

## common taboo targets

### philosophical terms
consciousness, free will, identity, meaning, truth, reality, self

### political terms

### evaluative terms
good, bad, art, intelligence, success, healthy, natural

### technical terms used loosely
AI, algorithm, data, science, evidence, proof

### business/org terms

---

## anti-patterns

### synonym substitution
replacing "freedom" with "liberty" is not taboo — you need to unpack what you're actually tracking.

### definition recitation
giving a dictionary definition misses the point — taboo asks what *you* mean, in *this* context.

### scope creep
if unpacking one term requires unpacking five others, focus on the most load-bearing term first.

### weaponization
taboo is for clarity, not for making opponents look confused. apply it to your own terms first.

## examples

### example 1: taboo "consciousness"

**context:** debate about whether AI systems are conscious

**ban:** "consciousness" cannot appear

**party A unpacked:**
"I'm tracking whether there's subjective experience — something it's *like* to be the system. specifically, whether information integration produces unified phenomenal states that the system can introspect on."

**party B unpacked:**
"I'm tracking behavioral markers — does it report internal states, model itself, exhibit preferences that suggest self-representation? I'm agnostic about 'inner experience' because I don't know how to test for that."

**revealed:**
- A is making a metaphysical claim about phenomenology
- B is making a functional/behavioral claim
- they were never disagreeing about the same thing

**residual disagreement:**
"does phenomenal experience matter morally independent of behavioral function?"

---

### example 2: taboo "fair"

**context:** discussion about whether a hiring process is fair

**ban:** "fair" cannot appear

**unpacked version 1:**
"the process should select candidates based only on job-relevant qualifications, without considering demographic factors."

**unpacked version 2:**
"the process should produce outcomes that reflect the demographic distribution of qualified applicants."

**unpacked version 3:**
"the process should correct for historical disadvantages by giving additional weight to underrepresented groups."

**revealed:**
- three completely different criteria bundled under one word
- disagreement is about values and tradeoffs, not about "fairness"
- the word was obscuring genuine normative conflict

---

### example 3: taboo "intelligent"

**context:** "is GPT-4 intelligent?"

**ban:** "intelligent" cannot appear

**unpacked:**
"does GPT-4:
- solve novel problems it wasn't explicitly trained on? [partially yes]
- form accurate world models? [unclear/contested]
- exhibit goal-directed behavior? [debatable framing]
- understand what it's doing? [requires tabooing 'understand']
- have the cognitive flexibility of humans? [no, different architecture]"

**revealed:**
- "intelligent" was bundling 5+ distinct properties
- the question dissolves into specific empirical/definitional questions
- some components are clearly yes, some clearly no, some undefined

## meta-note

taboo = **semantic hygiene**.

it doesn't resolve disagreements — it reveals whether disagreements are real. half of all "deep philosophical disputes" evaporate when you force people to say what they mean.

the rationalist origin: Eliezer Yudkowsky's "Taboo Your Words" sequence on LessWrong, derived from the logical positivist intuition that most metaphysical confusion is linguistic confusion in disguise.
