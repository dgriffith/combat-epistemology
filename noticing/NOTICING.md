---
name: noticing
description: noticing is the meta-skill of catching yourself glossing over confusion, discomfort, or inconsistency. it's the moment before rationalization kicks in — noticing the flicker of "wait, that doesn't add up" before the brain smooths it over. the goal is to flag these moments, because the thing you're glossing over is often where the error or insight lives.
---

## tl;dr

**noticing** asks: "what am I glossing over right now?"

it catches the moment of confusion or discomfort before rationalization smooths it over.

> "that flicker of 'wait, something's off' — catch it before your brain explains it away"

it's a **confusion detection operator**.

---

## when to use

use **noticing** when:

- something feels **slightly off** but you can't articulate what
- you're about to **move past something** without examining it
- an explanation **should be satisfying** but isn't quite
- you find yourself **wanting to change the subject**
- there's an **ugh field** — a topic you flinch away from

don't use when:

- you've already noticed and need to investigate → **excavate**, **doublecrux**
- you need to stress-test a plan → **murphyjitsu**
- you're querying intuition → **innerloop**

rule of thumb:
**noticing = "what's the thing I'm about to not think about?"**

---

## the mechanism

the brain is a confabulation machine. when it encounters confusion, inconsistency, or discomfort, it typically:

1. **smooths over** — generates a plausible-sounding explanation
2. **moves on** — directs attention elsewhere
3. **forgets** — the moment of confusion doesn't reach memory

this happens in milliseconds. noticing is the skill of catching step 1 before it completes.

the insight: confusion is a signal, not a bug. when something doesn't add up and your brain is trying to make it add up, that's often where the interesting thing is hiding.

## what to notice

### epistemic signals
- **confusion** — "I don't actually understand this"
- **inconsistency** — "these two things don't fit together"
- **surprise** — "I didn't expect that"
- **too-convenient** — "that explanation works too well"
- **motivated reasoning** — "I really want this to be true"

### emotional signals
- **discomfort** — something feels wrong
- **flinch** — not wanting to look at something
- **relief** — suspiciously happy to accept an explanation
- **anger** — "how dare they" (often masks fear or shame)
- **boredom** — sometimes protective, sometimes real

### social signals
- **politeness override** — not saying what you actually think
- **consensus pull** — going along with the group
- **status awareness** — adjusting beliefs for appearance
- **avoiding conflict** — dropping a true point to keep peace

### process signals
- **rushing** — moving fast to avoid thinking
- **changing the subject** — pivoting away from something
- **false clarity** — pretending to understand more than you do
- **explanation cascade** — piling on reasons (sign of weak core)

## signature

noticing(situation) → {noticed_signal, what_was_glossed, why_glossing, decision}

- **situation:** what you're currently thinking about or doing

output is:

- **noticed_signal** — what type of signal was caught
- **what_was_glossed** — the thing you were about to skip
- **why_glossing** — what the brain was protecting against
- **decision** — investigate now, note for later, or consciously move on

## process (step by step)

### step 0: build the meta-attention
noticing is a background skill, not a foreground process. the goal is to have a part of attention watching for signals while you work/think normally.

practice prompts:
- pause occasionally and ask "what am I not thinking about?"
- after reaching a conclusion, ask "what did I skip?"
- when relieved, ask "what was I afraid I'd find?"

### step 1: catch the flicker
signals often appear as:
- a slight sensation of "huh" or "wait"
- physical discomfort (tension, looking away)
- a desire to move on quickly
- relief when an explanation appears

the key is catching these before they're overwritten.

### step 2: name what you noticed
don't try to explain it yet. just name it:
- "I noticed confusion about X"
- "I noticed I want to skip Y"
- "I noticed that explanation felt too easy"

naming prevents the smoothing-over.

### step 3: resist the first explanation
your brain will immediately offer an explanation. hold it lightly.

ask: "is that actually what's going on, or is that my brain's cover story?"

the first explanation is often a protective rationalization.

### step 4: investigate or flag
decide:
- **investigate now** — if it seems important, dig in
- **flag for later** — note it; return when you have capacity
- **consciously release** — not everything needs investigation, but do it explicitly

the anti-pattern is implicit release: not investigating and not admitting you chose not to.

---

## quality criteria

**genuine noticing**
- [ ] caught the signal before complete smoothing-over
- [ ] named the thing being glossed, not just "something feels off"
- [ ] distinguished the signal from noise

**honest examination**
- [ ] didn't immediately accept the first explanation
- [ ] considered that the discomfort might be pointing at something real
- [ ] checked for motivated reasoning

**appropriate response**
- [ ] made an explicit choice about whether to investigate
- [ ] if not investigating, acknowledged what you're leaving unexplored
- [ ] didn't just forget about it

## the ugh field

a special case: topics that generate strong aversive reactions just by thinking about them.

signs of an ugh field:
- you flinch away from the topic
- you've been "meaning to deal with that"
- you feel tired when it comes up
- you generate excuses not to engage

ugh fields often surround:
- avoided tasks (taxes, difficult emails)
- unresolved problems (relationship issues, health concerns)
- threatening questions (what if I'm wrong about this?)
- identity-challenging topics

noticing ugh fields is the first step to addressing them. the field is a signal that something important is being protected.

## anti-patterns

### explanation too fast
"I felt confused but then realized it's because X" — did you actually figure it out, or did your brain generate a plausible cover?

### meta-bypass
"I noticed I'm noticing, so I'm doing it right" — noticing is useful when it points at something real, not as an end in itself.

### over-noticing
treating every minor signal as significant. some confusion is just confusion; some discomfort is just discomfort.

### noticing as avoidance
"I'm still noticing things, I'm not ready to act yet" — at some point, you have to engage with what you've noticed.

### performance noticing
narrating your noticing to others instead of actually using it.

## examples

### example 1: noticing in a meeting

**situation:** listening to a proposal from a colleague

**signal caught:** slight "huh" when they described the user research

**what was glossed:** they said "users want X" but I only remember them testing with 3 people

**first explanation offered:** "well, they're the expert on this"

**investigation:**
- the sample size is too small to support the conclusion
- but I don't want to challenge them publicly
- there's social cost to questioning

**decision:** flag for one-on-one follow-up. ask "how confident are we in the user research?" without implying criticism

---

### example 2: noticing while planning

**situation:** making a project plan

**signal caught:** slight tension when estimating the "integration phase"

**what was glossed:** wrote "2 weeks" without actually thinking about what integration involves

**first explanation offered:** "it's always about 2 weeks"

**investigation:**
- I don't actually know what integration will require
- I've been burned by this before
- I wrote 2 weeks because it sounds reasonable, not because I know

**decision:** investigate now. break down the integration into actual steps. probably 4-6 weeks.

---

### example 3: noticing in a difficult conversation

**situation:** partner says "it's fine, do whatever you want"

**signal caught:** relief, followed by tiny flicker of "wait"

**what was glossed:** that tone didn't match those words

**first explanation offered:** "they said it's fine, so it's fine"

**investigation:**
- they said fine but they're annoyed
- I'm taking the words at face value because it's easier
- if I acknowledge the tone, we'll have to talk about it

**decision:** don't take the escape hatch. "you're saying fine, but I'm sensing it's not actually fine. what's going on?"

---

### example 4: noticing about one's own beliefs

**situation:** reading an article that challenges my position

**signal caught:** sudden tiredness and desire to close the tab

**what was glossed:** the article made a point I don't have a good response to

**first explanation offered:** "it's poorly written" / "they're arguing in bad faith"

**investigation:**
- is the article actually poor, or do I just not like it?
- the specific paragraph I closed on — what did it say?
- am I flinching because it might be right?

**decision:** reopen. read the paragraph. sit with the discomfort. this might be a crux.

## building the skill

noticing is a background skill that develops with practice:

**daily practice:**
- at end of day, ask: "what did I gloss over today?"
- review decisions: "where did I move fast without thinking?"

**in conversation:**
- notice when you want to change the subject
- notice when you're formulating response instead of listening
- notice when you agree to end a discussion

**in reading/learning:**
- notice when you're skimming
- notice when "I'll come back to that" means "I'll never come back to that"
- notice the paragraph you want to skip

**somatic awareness:**
- tension often precedes cognitive noticing
- where in your body does discomfort show up?

## meta-note

noticing = **attention to attention**.

it's the moment before the brain smooths things over. that flicker of confusion, discomfort, or "wait" is often more honest than the explanation that follows. catching these moments is how you find bugs in your thinking — and occasionally, breakthroughs.

the rationalist origin: developed across LessWrong and CFAR as a core epistemic skill, building on mindfulness traditions but specifically oriented toward cognitive error detection. the "ugh field" concept comes from LessWrong user Alicorn.
