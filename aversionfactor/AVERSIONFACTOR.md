---
name: aversionfactor
description: aversionfactor surfaces the hidden objection beneath the stated one. when someone says "I can't do X because Y," the real blocker is often not Y. this operation digs beneath surface-level resistance to find the actual aversion — fear of failure, identity threat, loss of optionality, social risk — that's generating the "no." once the real aversion is visible, it can be addressed directly or worked around.
---

## tl;dr

**aversionfactor** asks: "what's the real objection hiding beneath the stated one?"

it surfaces hidden resistance by repeatedly asking "and if that weren't true, would you do it?"

> stated: "I don't have time"
> real: "I'm afraid I'll fail and that would be humiliating"

it's a **resistance decomposition operator**.

---

## when to use

use **aversionfactor** when:

- someone (including yourself) keeps **not doing** something they claim to want
- objections seem to **shift** when addressed ("okay, but also...")
- the stated reason feels like a **rationalization**
- you've solved the practical problems but action still doesn't happen
- there's an **ugh field** around a topic — a felt sense of "I don't want to think about this"

don't use when:

- the stated objection is genuine and sufficient → just solve it
- you're exploring goals, not resistance → **goalfactor**
- you want to surface assumptions → **excavate**

rule of thumb:
**aversionfactor = "if that problem were magically solved, would you do it? no? then what's the real reason?"**

---

## the mechanism

stated objections are often not the real objections. the real objections are:

1. **too threatening to voice** — "I'm afraid of failing" is harder to say than "I'm too busy"
2. **not consciously accessible** — the person genuinely doesn't know what's blocking them
3. **multiply determined** — several aversions stack up, but only one gets verbalized

aversionfactor works by:

1. **the magic wand test** — "if [stated objection] were magically solved, would you do it?"
2. **iterative peeling** — each "no" reveals another layer
3. **aversion taxonomy** — categorizing what type of resistance this is
4. **direct addressing** — engaging with the real aversion, not the proxy

the insight: most procrastination and "stuck" patterns are protection against something. finding what's being protected reveals what's actually going on.

---

## signature

aversionfactor(stated_objection, desired_action) → {aversion_stack, root_aversion, aversion_type, resolution_paths}

- **stated_objection:** the surface-level reason given ("I don't have time")
- **desired_action:** what they're not doing but claim to want

output is:

- **aversion_stack** — layers of objection from surface to root
- **root_aversion** — the deepest resistance
- **aversion_type** — category of the root aversion
- **resolution_paths** — ways to address or work around the real aversion

## process (step by step)

### step 0: identify the pattern
notice: what action is not happening despite stated intention? what reason is given?

### step 1: apply the magic wand test
"imagine [stated objection] were completely solved — you had infinite time/money/skill/whatever. would you do it then?"

- if **yes** → the stated objection might be genuine; help solve it
- if **hesitation or no** → there's a deeper aversion; continue

### step 2: surface the next objection
"so if time weren't the issue, what would still make this hard?"

capture the new objection. don't judge it.

### step 3: iterate the magic wand
repeat step 1-2 on the new objection. continue until:
- you hit something that feels like the real resistance
- the person says "yeah, if that were solved, I'd actually do it"

### step 4: classify the root aversion
what type of resistance is this? (see taxonomy below)

### step 5: validate the aversion
the aversion exists for a reason. acknowledge it:
- "it makes sense that you'd be afraid of [X]"
- "that's a real risk/cost you're protecting against"

validation isn't agreement — it's recognition that the aversion is doing something.

### step 6: explore resolution paths
now that the real aversion is visible:
- can it be addressed directly?
- can the action be modified to reduce the aversion?
- can the aversion be accepted and acted through anyway?
- is the aversion revealing that you don't actually want the thing?

## aversion taxonomy

### fear-based aversions

**fear of failure**
"if I try and fail, that means I'm not good enough"
- reveals: identity is attached to outcome
- resolution: decouple identity from outcome, reframe failure as data

**fear of success**
"if I succeed, expectations will rise / my life will change / I'll lose something"
- reveals: success has hidden costs
- resolution: examine what success would actually change, address the real fear

**fear of judgment**
"people will think I'm [arrogant/stupid/selfish]"
- reveals: social self is threatened
- resolution: whose judgment actually matters? what's the realistic consequence?

**fear of commitment**
"if I do this, I'm closing off other options"
- reveals: optionality is being protected
- resolution: is the optionality actually valuable? opportunity cost of not choosing?

### identity-based aversions

**"that's not who I am"**
"doing X would mean I'm the kind of person who..."
- reveals: action conflicts with self-image
- resolution: is the self-image serving you? can you expand it?

**competence threat**
"I should already know how to do this; asking for help is shameful"
- reveals: incompetence is identity-threatening
- resolution: separate learning from failing; normalize not-knowing

### effort-based aversions

**activation energy**
"I know it's not that hard but I just... can't start"
- reveals: the first step feels aversive even if the task isn't
- resolution: make the first step trivially small; address environmental friction

**anticipated effort mismatch**
"it seems like so much work" (but when examined, it's not)
- reveals: the task has accrued negative associations
- resolution: break down actual steps; do one step to break the spell

### social aversions

**social exposure**
"I'd have to [ask for help / promote myself / be visible]"
- reveals: social risk is the real cost
- resolution: what's the actual worst case? can you do a smaller exposure?

**relationship threat**
"this might upset [person] / change our dynamic"
- reveals: relationship stability is being protected
- resolution: is the protection necessary? what's the cost of not acting?

### values-based aversions

**moral discomfort**
"this feels ethically gray / it's not how things should be done"
- reveals: action conflicts with values (possibly legitimate!)
- resolution: is the discomfort tracking something real? adjust action or accept tradeoff?

**authenticity violation**
"this would be fake / manipulative / not genuine"
- reveals: action conflicts with authenticity values
- resolution: is there a version of the action that feels authentic?

## quality criteria

**excavation depth**
- [ ] used the magic wand test at least 3 times
- [ ] reached an aversion that feels like the real one
- [ ] person resonates with the identified root aversion

**aversion validation**
- [ ] acknowledged the aversion without dismissing it
- [ ] recognized what the aversion is protecting against
- [ ] avoided "you just need to push through" dismissiveness

**resolution appropriateness**
- [ ] resolution paths address the actual aversion, not the stated objection
- [ ] considered that the aversion might be correct (the action might be wrong)
- [ ] offered multiple paths, not just "get over it"

## anti-patterns

### surface-level problem solving
"you say you don't have time? let's optimize your calendar!"
→ solving the stated objection when it's a proxy wastes effort

### judgment
"that's a dumb reason not to do it"
→ shaming the aversion drives it underground; it'll re-emerge as a different objection

### forced positivity
"you'll definitely succeed! there's nothing to be afraid of!"
→ dismissing legitimate fears doesn't address them

### one-size-fits-all
"you just need more motivation/discipline/accountability"
→ generic solutions don't address specific aversions

### premature resolution
jumping to solutions before fully understanding the aversion
→ take time to sit with what's actually there

## integration with other ops

**upstream:**
- **goalfactor** → "do you actually want this?" should come before "why aren't you doing it?"

**downstream:**
- **murphyjitsu** → once aversion is addressed, stress-test the plan
- **doublecrux** → if the aversion is about a belief ("I'll definitely fail"), find the crux

**parallel:**
- **taboo** → if the aversion is vague ("it's just hard"), force unpacking

## examples

### example 1: aversionfactor "I don't have time to exercise"

**stated objection:** "I don't have time"

**magic wand #1:** "if you had 2 extra hours per day, would you exercise?"
→ "well... probably, but I'd still be so tired"

**magic wand #2:** "if you had time AND weren't tired?"
→ "I guess... but I don't really like going to the gym, everyone there is already fit"

**magic wand #3:** "if you could exercise somewhere private?"
→ "...yeah, I'd probably do it then. I just feel embarrassed because I'm so out of shape"

**aversion stack:**
1. "I don't have time" (stated)
2. "I'm too tired" (second layer)
3. "I don't like the gym environment" (third layer)
4. "I feel embarrassed about my body/fitness level" (root)

**root aversion:** fear of judgment / social exposure

**resolution paths:**
- home workouts (removes exposure)
- early morning gym when it's empty
- find a beginner-friendly environment
- work with a trainer one-on-one
- reframe: everyone at the gym started somewhere; they respect you for being there

---

### example 2: aversionfactor "I can't leave my job because of the economy"

**stated objection:** "the job market is bad right now"

**magic wand #1:** "if the economy were booming and jobs were easy to get?"
→ "I'd still need to make sure it's the right move. I've been here a long time"

**magic wand #2:** "if you knew a great job was waiting for you?"
→ "I'd worry about what my colleagues would think. they've invested in me"

**magic wand #3:** "if your colleagues would be supportive and happy for you?"
→ "...then yeah, I think I'd go. but they'd feel like I abandoned them"

**aversion stack:**
1. "economy is bad" (stated)
2. "need to find the right opportunity" (second layer)
3. "worried about colleagues' reaction" (third layer)
4. "fear of being seen as disloyal / abandoning people" (root)

**root aversion:** relationship threat / identity as loyal person

**resolution paths:**
- examine if the loyalty is mutual (does company feel loyal to you?)
- separate loyalty to people from loyalty to institution
- consider: what would colleagues actually say? (likely supportive)
- what's the cost of staying out of misplaced loyalty?

---

### example 3: aversionfactor "I should write that blog post but I keep not doing it"

**stated objection:** "I haven't found the time to sit down and write"

**magic wand #1:** "if you had a full day blocked off just for writing?"
→ "I'd probably spend it researching more. I don't feel like I have enough to say"

**magic wand #2:** "if you were confident you had valuable things to say?"
→ "I'd worry it wouldn't be good enough. other people have written about this better"

**magic wand #3:** "if you knew it would be well-received?"
→ "...I'd still worry about being criticized. what if people disagree?"

**aversion stack:**
1. "no time" (stated)
2. "need more research" (second layer)
3. "others have said it better" (third layer)
4. "fear of criticism / public intellectual exposure" (root)

**root aversion:** fear of judgment + competence threat

**resolution paths:**
- write under a pseudonym to decouple identity from reception
- write for yourself first, decide later whether to publish
- set a minimum bar: "useful to at least one person" vs "definitive treatment"
- expose to small audience first (trusted friends)
- accept: all published writing gets some criticism; that's not failure

## meta-note

aversionfactor = **resistance archaeology**.

it finds what's actually generating the "no" — which is often something much more interesting and addressable than the stated reason. the stated objection is usually a bodyguard protecting the real vulnerability.

the CFAR origin: developed as "aversion factoring" in the CFAR curriculum, based on the observation that humans systematically misreport their objections, often because the real objections are emotionally costly to voice or aren't consciously accessible.
